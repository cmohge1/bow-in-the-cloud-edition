If in time, and worth using, they are heartily at thy Service. Sorry only, if it could have lent one somewhat worthier of the acceptance, but if they serve to show that I wish, well to the effort, the object they have in view and end is answered. In the spirit of the annexed trifle I have endeavor'd briefly, to express some of those better feelings which I cannot but think the common character of true Christians & Saints of whatever name or profession in the unfortunate Slaves, Mother, in reference to her equally unfortunate Child. But as all believe in Slaves now, would they were more frequent & in which Humanity, the latter of Humanities be the consolation of the oppressed & are forced to Suffer injury & Death Being, I have en- deavour'd in the second short trifle to embody some of those humble feelings of a Negro Mother still. I did without compromise of the principle. Surely the sentiment contained in the concluding Verse must occur to such as one. For in my own apprehension as is the Company of Humanity, Sympathy & the Glory of the Endeavour, the Welfare & the Lot which he is the highest Victim, implies a thraldom yet not more dreadful –– Thine respectfully B. B. Sleep, my child! and might the prayer Of thy mother's dark despair Be accepted for thy sake,- 'Twere that thou no more shouldst wake, Though a mother's love be mine, And a daughter's fondness thine, Yet, for thee, a parent's breath Craves the boon of early death. Worse to live a helpless slave, Than to fill an early grave; Better far the silent tomb, Than the captive's hopeless doom. White man's cruelty and lust Cannot harm the lifeless dust; Powerless the oppressor's rod, Brandished o'er a senseless clod. Ruthless lash, and galling chain, Ceaseless tasks-performed with pain, Nights of sorrow, days of toil-- These have made my life their spoil. Such, with life, must be thy lot; Dying-thou shalt know them not;- O, be thine, all fetters breaking, Sleep that knows on earth no waking!
