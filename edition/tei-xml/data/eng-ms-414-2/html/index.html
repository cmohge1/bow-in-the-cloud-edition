If in time, and worth using, they are heartily at thy Service. I only wish I could have sent thee somewhat worthier of thy acceptance, but if these serve to show that I wish well to thy efforts, & the object they have in view one end is answered. In the first of the annexed trifles I have endeavor'd briefly to express some of those better feelings which I cannot but think the common dictates of Nature must excite in the unfortunate Slave Mother, in reference to her equally miserable Child. But as I believe instances occur, would they were more frequent, in which through the labours of Missionaries &c the Consolations of the Gospel are opened to those injured & devoted Beings, I have en- deavour'd in the second short article to embody some of the thoughts & feelings of a Converted Negro –– still, I hope, without compromise of the principle. Surely the sentiment contained in the concluding Verse must occur to such an one –– for in my view deplorable as is the Bondage of the unhappy Negro the Slavery of the Enslaver, the hopeless yoke of which he is the bigoted Victim, implies a thraldom yet not more dreadful –– Thine respectfully B. B. A Negro Mother's Cradle Song. Sleep, my child! and might the prayer Of thy Mother's dark despair Be accepted for thy sake,–– 'Twere that thou no more shouldst wake, Though a Mother's Love be mine, And a Daughter's fondness thine, Yet for thee A Parent's breath Craves the boon of early death. Worse to live a helpless Slave, Than to fill an early grave; Better far the silent tomb, Than the Captive's hopeless doom. White Man's cruelty and lust Cannot harm the lifeless dust; Powerless the Oppressor's rod Brandish'd o'er a senseless clod. Ruthless lash, and galling chain, Ceaseless tasks -– perform'd with pain, Nights of sorrow, days of toil-- These have made my life their spoil. Such, with Life, must be thy Lot; Dying –– thou shalt know them not; –– O be thine, all fetters breaking, Sleep that knows on earth no waking! A Christian Negro's thanks, and Prayer. O Father of the human race! The white, the black, the bond, the free;–– Thanks for thy Gift of heavenly Grace, Vouchsaf'd, through Jesus Christ, to me. This, 'mid Oppression's every wrong, Has borne my sinking spirit up; Made sorrow joyful, -- weakness strong, And sweetened Slavery's bitter cup. Hath not A Saviour's dying hour Made e'en the yoke of thraldom light? Hath not Thy Holy Spirit's power Made bondage freedom; -- darkness bright? Thanks, then, O Father! for the gift, Which through Thy Gospel Thou hast given; Which thus from bonds and earth can lift The Soul to liberty and Heaven. But not the less I mourn their shame, Who, mindless of Thy gracious Will, Call on A Father's -- Saviour's Name, Yet keep their Brethren Bondsmen still! Forgive them, Lord! for Jesus' sake; And since The Slave Thou hast unbound,-- The chains which bind The Oppressor -- break, And be Thy Love's last triumph crowned! B. B.
